## 1. Business Requirements Gathering

> **Focus:** Discovery questions, scope management, user stories for analytics projects, prototyping dashboards, feedback loops. How to elicit what the business actually needs (not what they say they want).

### 1.1 Build Products That Solve Real Problems With This Lightweight JTBD Framework

- **URL:** https://firstround.com/review/build-products-that-solve-real-problems-with-this-lightweight-jtbd-framework/
- **Type:** Article
- **Author:** Sunita Mohanty (Product Lead, Facebook NPE; former Oculus, Lumosity)
- **Note:** Provides a concrete, battle-tested JTBD template (`When I… / But… / Help me… / So I…`) with real-world examples from Discord, Peloton, Segment, and Mutiny — directly applicable to framing analytics requirements around real user jobs.
- **Approximate time:** ~25 min read

### 1.2 Are You Solving the Right Problem?

- **URL:** https://hbr.org/2012/09/are-you-solving-the-right-problem
- **Type:** Article (Harvard Business Review)
- **Author:** Dwayne Spradlin (CEO, InnoCentive)
- **Note:** The classic HBR piece arguing that problem definition rigor is the single most important factor in finding good solutions — opens with Einstein's "If I had an hour to save the planet, I would spend 59 minutes defining the problem."
- **Approximate time:** ~10 min read

### 1.3 Know Your Customers' "Jobs to Be Done"

- **URL:** https://hbr.org/2016/09/know-your-customers-jobs-to-be-done
- **Type:** Article (Harvard Business Review)
- **Author:** Clayton Christensen, Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David S. Duncan
- **Note:** The seminal JTBD article — explains the "milkshake" example and teaches how understanding why customers "hire" a product/service helps you separate what stakeholders say they want from what they actually need.
- **Approximate time:** ~20 min read

### 1.4 SWD Podcast Episode 19: "Ask Smart Questions"

- **URL:** https://www.storytellingwithdata.com/podcast/ask-smart-questions
- **Type:** Podcast (storytelling with data)
- **Author:** Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic
- **Note:** Covers 5 discovery questions for exploring data and 5 for explaining it, plus a dedicated listener Q&A segment on **"How do I collect requirements to create a powerful visualization?"** — frames the analyst's job as being a consultant, not an order-taker.
- **Approximate time:** ~35 min listen

## 2. Working with Ambiguity

> **Focus:** Making defensible assumptions, triangulating from incomplete data, knowing when analysis is "done enough," building trust through documented work. NZ context is valuable — government data is notoriously fragmented.

### 2.1 How Not to Be Stupid

- **URL:** https://fs.blog/how-not-to-be-stupid/
- **Type:** Article/conversation (Farnam Street)
- **Author:** Adam Robinson (interviewed by Shane Parrish)
- **Note:** Defines stupidity as "overlooking or dismissing conspicuously crucial information" and identifies the 7 situational factors (including rushing, information overload, being outside your circle of competence) that lead to it — essential self-awareness framework for anyone working with fragmented or ambiguous data.
- **Approximate time:** ~10 min read

### 2.2 How to Make Smart Decisions Without Getting Lucky

- **URL:** https://fs.blog/smart-decisions/
- **Type:** Guide/article collection (Farnam Street)
- **Author:** Shane Parrish
- **Note:** Comprehensive overview of decision-making under uncertainty using mental models — covers probabilistic thinking, inversion ("what would guarantee failure?"), second-order thinking, and when to stop analyzing and act. Links to dozens of deeper articles.
- **Approximate time:** ~30 min read (main guide); extensive rabbit-hole potential

### 2.3 Annie Duke's Substack (Free Newsletter)

- **URL:** https://www.annieduke.com/substack/
- **Type:** Newsletter (free tier)
- **Author:** Annie Duke (former poker champion, decision scientist)
- **Note:** Regular, practical essays on decision-making under uncertainty. Recent topics include "Terrified of Small Bets," "Making Better Decisions in a Data-Rich World," and "Acknowledging and Navigating Uncertainty." Duke's core framework is directly applicable to making defensible assumptions when data is incomplete.
- **Approximate time:** ~5–10 min per post, delivered to inbox

### 2.4 "Thinking in Bets" — Annie Duke at Google (YouTube)

- **URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JhY1y4chI3Q
- **Type:** Video/talk (YouTube)
- **Author:** Annie Duke
- **Note:** Duke explains how to make smarter decisions when you don't have all the facts — core to triangulating from incomplete data. Introduces the concept of "resulting" (judging decision quality by outcome rather than process) and how to build trust through documented reasoning.
- **Approximate time:** ~45 min watch

### B.2 NZ Government Data Context — Applied Principles

When working with fragmented government data in New Zealand (Stats NZ, MBIE, MoH, MSD, etc.), consider these principles drawn from the resources above:

- **Triangulate across sources** (Duke's probabilistic thinking): No single dataset tells the full story. Use multiple imperfect sources to build confidence, and document which source said what.
- **Map your assumptions** (Robinson's 7 factors): Government data often has different collection methodologies, refresh cycles, and definitions across agencies. Explicitly list every assumption you're making so stakeholders understand the terrain.
- **Define "done enough" upfront** (Christensen's JTBD): Ask "What decision will this analysis inform?" — the required precision is different for a ministerial briefing than for an operational dashboard.
- **Build trust through process transparency** (Parrish's decision-making guide): When data is fragmented, stakeholders trust you based on the visible rigor of your method, not on the cleanliness of results.

The SWD podcast catalog is an excellent ongoing resource — many episodes address real-world data messiness. Full episode list: https://www.storytellingwithdata.com/podcast
